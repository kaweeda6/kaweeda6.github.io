Dental implants are the gold standard for replacing missing teeth — but one of the first questions patients ask is, *how much is this actually going to cost me?* We believe in full transparency, so here's an honest breakdown of what implants cost at our Bayside practice and throughout Queens, including the factors that affect pricing, insurance realities, and how different treatment options change the number.

## What's the Average Cost of a Single Dental Implant in Queens, NY?

In the New York City metro area, a single dental implant — including the implant post, abutment, and crown — typically ranges from **$3,500 to $6,000**. At Thrive Dental in Bayside, our all-inclusive implant pricing falls within this range and is reviewed individually at each consultation with a complete, itemized breakdown.

That number can feel significant, but it's worth understanding what you're paying for — and why implants are often *more* cost-effective than alternatives over a lifetime.

## What Does the Implant Price Include?

A complete implant restoration has three parts:

1. **The implant post** — a titanium screw that is surgically placed into the jawbone. This is the "root" of your new tooth, and the component that makes an implant fundamentally different from every other tooth replacement option.
2. **The abutment** — a small connector piece that attaches to the post above the gumline and supports the crown.
3. **The crown** — the custom-fabricated visible tooth, color-matched and shaped precisely to blend with your surrounding teeth.

At Thrive Dental, we handle all three components in-house with our own team and iTero digital scanner, which means fewer appointments, better precision, and no lab coordination delays.

**What is typically not included in the base implant price:**
- Initial consultation and 3D cone beam CT scan (sometimes included; we'll tell you upfront)
- Bone grafting, if needed
- Tooth extraction, if the failing tooth is still in place
- Sinus lift, for certain upper jaw cases

We always provide a complete written estimate before treatment begins — no surprises at the end.

## What Can Affect the Total Cost?

Several factors can significantly raise or lower your total investment:

### Bone Grafting

If you've had a missing tooth for a while, the jawbone naturally resorbs (shrinks) in the area where the root used to be. If bone density is insufficient to support the implant post, a bone graft is performed first. Depending on the extent of bone loss:

- **Minor graft** (socket preservation at time of extraction): $300–$600
- **Moderate graft** (particulate graft placed before implant): $800–$1,500
- **Significant graft** (block graft from donor site or patient's own bone): $1,500–$3,000+

Many patients told at other offices that bone loss makes implants impossible find that grafting makes them viable. We evaluate every case with a 3D cone beam scan.

### Sinus Lift

The upper back teeth sit directly below the sinus cavity. When bone has been lost in this area, the sinus may need to be "lifted" to create adequate vertical space for the implant. A sinus augmentation typically adds **$1,500–$3,500** to the total cost, depending on whether it's a lateral window approach or a more conservative osteotome technique.

### Tooth Extraction

If the tooth being replaced is still present (failed root canal, fractured tooth, severe decay), extraction is a separate step performed before implant placement. Expect **$150–$400** for a simple extraction, **$300–$600** for a surgical extraction.

### Number of Implants and Placement Location

Front teeth and back teeth have different complexity profiles. Back teeth absorb greater chewing forces and typically require more bone volume, which can affect cost. Multiple adjacent missing teeth may be replaced with an implant-supported bridge (fewer implants than teeth), which changes the cost structure.

## All-on-4 and Full-Arch Implant Pricing

When a patient is missing all or most teeth in an arch, individual implants for every tooth aren't necessary — or cost-effective. The **All-on-4** protocol uses four strategically placed implants to support a full arch of teeth (12–14 teeth), dramatically reducing both cost and procedure time compared to placing 12 individual implants.

At Thrive Dental and at comparable practices in the Queens area:

| Treatment | Approximate Cost Per Arch |
|---|---|
| All-on-4 (fixed full arch) | $20,000–$30,000 per arch |
| All-on-6 (additional stability) | $24,000–$35,000 per arch |
| Implant-supported overdenture (removable) | $8,000–$15,000 per arch |
| Traditional full denture (no implants) | $1,500–$3,000 per arch |

All-on-4 is a significant investment — but for patients who would otherwise wear full dentures, the functional and quality-of-life difference is dramatic. Unlike conventional dentures, All-on-4 restorations are fixed (they don't come out), preserve jawbone, and allow normal eating and speech. Over a 15–20 year lifespan, the cost-per-year is often comparable to maintaining and replacing traditional dentures.

## Does Dental Insurance Cover Implants?

This is the most common question — and the honest answer is: *sometimes, partially.*

Most traditional dental insurance plans classify implants as "elective" rather than "medically necessary" and either exclude them entirely or cover only specific components. Here's what to look for:

- **Bone graft or extraction**: Often covered at the plan's surgical benefit rate (50–80% of covered fee), even when the implant itself isn't
- **Implant crown**: Some PPO plans cover the crown as a prosthetic item (similar to a bridge pontic) at 50%
- **Implant post (the surgery)**: Least likely to be covered; many plans explicitly exclude osseointegrated implants
- **Annual maximum**: Most dental plans cap annual benefits at $1,000–$2,000, which is quickly exhausted by implant treatment

**What to do:** Call your insurance company directly and ask: *"Does my plan cover dental implants, implant crowns, or bone grafts? What is the covered fee schedule and what percentage does my plan pay after deductible?"* We also submit a **pre-authorization** before any treatment begins so you have a written estimate from your insurer before committing.

## Does Medicare or Medicaid Cover Dental Implants in New York?

**Medicare (Parts A and B)** does not cover routine dental care, including implants. Original Medicare has a longstanding exclusion for dental services. Some **Medicare Advantage** (Part C) plans include limited dental benefits — a small number cover implant crowns, though coverage varies widely by plan. If you have Medicare Advantage, check your Evidence of Coverage booklet or call your plan administrator.

**Medicaid in New York** covers basic dental services (extractions, fillings, some dentures) but does not cover dental implants as of the current benefit structure. Patients relying on Medicaid for dental coverage are typically limited to tooth extractions and conventional removable dentures.

## What About Mini Implants?

Mini implants are narrower-diameter implants (less than 3mm, vs. 3.5–5mm for standard implants) that require less bone and can sometimes be placed without grafting. They cost less per implant — typically **$1,000–$2,500 per mini implant** — and are often marketed as a budget alternative.

The tradeoff: mini implants are significantly less durable under load, are not recommended for supporting crowns on back teeth where bite forces are highest, and have a lower long-term success rate than standard implants. At Thrive Dental, we use standard-diameter implants because the long-term outcomes are simply better. We'll tell you if a case is appropriate for a narrower implant — but we won't recommend mini implants as a cost-cutting measure when a standard implant is the right clinical choice.

## Why Do Implant Prices Vary So Much Between Practices?

A search for "dental implants Queens" will return prices ranging from $799 "per implant" to $7,000 for a complete case. This variation is almost always explained by one of these factors:

- **What's included**: A $799 price typically covers only the implant post — not the abutment, not the crown, not the consultation or imaging. Always ask for the all-in price.
- **Imaging technology**: Practices using 3D cone beam CT for implant planning provide dramatically more precise placement — and that equipment and training carries a cost. Practices placing implants based on 2D X-rays alone are cutting a corner that directly affects outcomes.
- **In-house vs. referred placement**: Some general dentists refer surgical placement to an oral surgeon, adding a separate fee. We place and restore implants in-house, which reduces total cost and coordination.
- **Lab fabrication quality**: The crown matters. A cheaper off-shore lab crown looks and wears differently than a premium domestic laboratory crown matched precisely to your bite.
- **Geographic pricing**: Manhattan practices charge significantly more than Queens practices for the same procedure. Our Bayside location reflects the lower real estate and overhead costs of Northeast Queens.

## How Do Implants Compare in Cost to Other Options?

The upfront cost of an implant is higher than a bridge or denture — but the lifetime cost tells a different story:

| Option | Upfront Cost | Typical Lifespan | 20-Year Cost Estimate |
|---|---|---|---|
| Dental implant | $3,500–$6,000 | Lifetime (post) | $3,500–$6,000 |
| Dental bridge | $2,500–$4,500 | 10–15 years | $5,000–$9,000+ |
| Removable partial denture | $1,000–$2,500 | 5–10 years | $3,000–$7,000+ |
| No replacement (leave gap) | $0 | — | High (bone loss, shifting teeth, future restorative costs) |

Bridges require grinding down healthy adjacent teeth to serve as anchors — which weakens those teeth and often leads to future crowns or root canals on the anchor teeth. Implants stand alone, preserve jawbone, and don't affect neighboring teeth.
