*"Every six months."* You have heard it your entire life. But where did that guideline actually come from — and is it right for you?

The twice-yearly recommendation traces back to a 1950s Pepsodent toothpaste marketing campaign, not a clinical study. The real answer is more nuanced, and it depends entirely on the state of your oral health.

## The Three Cleaning Schedules

### Every 6 Months — For Low-Risk Patients

Twice-yearly cleanings are appropriate if you:
- Have a consistent home care routine (brushing twice daily, flossing daily)
- Have no history of gum disease
- Have minimal plaque and tartar buildup
- Have no systemic conditions that affect oral health

For these patients, six-month intervals are sufficient to remove the buildup that daily brushing misses and to catch any early changes before they become problems.

### Every 3–4 Months — For Moderate to High-Risk Patients

More frequent cleanings are recommended if you:
- Have a history of gum disease (periodontitis), even if treated
- Are undergoing active periodontal therapy
- Have a high cavity rate or deep grooves in your teeth
- Smoke or use tobacco products
- Have dry mouth from medications, radiation, or other causes
- Have orthodontic appliances, bridges, or implants that trap plaque

Once you have had gum disease, the bacteria that cause it do not disappear — they just become harder to manage. Quarterly "periodontal maintenance" cleanings are clinically proven to significantly reduce the recurrence of gum disease and the associated bone loss.

### Annually — Rarely, But It Exists

Some studies suggest that very-low-risk patients with exceptional oral hygiene may only need one cleaning per year. This requires a careful clinical assessment and is typically a status you reach after years of excellent hygiene and clean checkups.

## What Actually Happens at a Cleaning?

A professional cleaning at Thrive Dental includes:

1. **Scaling** — removing calculus (hardened tartar) above and below the gumline using hand scalers and ultrasonic instruments
2. **Polishing** — removing surface stains and soft plaque with a slightly abrasive paste
3. **Flossing** — cleaning between teeth to remove any remaining debris
4. **Exam** — your dentist reviews X-rays, checks for cavities, evaluates gum health, and screens for oral cancer

For patients with gum disease, a deeper procedure called **scaling and root planing** (deep cleaning) may be performed under local anesthesia to clean below the gumline where a regular cleaning cannot reach.

## Why Skipping Cleanings Costs More

Here is the math patients often miss:

- A routine cleaning costs **$100–$250** (often fully covered by insurance)
- A deep cleaning (scaling and root planing) costs **$600–$1,200** per quadrant
- A filling costs **$150–$400** per tooth
- A root canal and crown costs **$2,000–$3,500** per tooth

Every year you skip a cleaning, you increase the probability of needing that more expensive downstream care. We have seen patients return after a 3-year gap who needed thousands of dollars of work that could have been prevented by a couple of routine cleanings.

## Warning Signs That You Need a Cleaning Sooner Than Scheduled

Even if your last cleaning was recent, a few symptoms warrant calling us before your next scheduled appointment:

- **Bleeding gums** when brushing or flossing — consistently, not just occasionally after aggressive brushing. Healthy gums should not bleed.
- **Persistent bad breath** that does not improve with brushing and mouthwash. This often indicates bacteria accumulating below the gumline.
- **Visible tartar buildup** (hard, yellowish or brownish deposits, usually behind the lower front teeth). Once tartar forms, you cannot remove it at home — only a dentist can.
- **Gum sensitivity or soreness** that has developed between visits.
- **A bad taste** in your mouth that does not resolve — this can indicate an abscess or active infection.

If you notice any of these between your scheduled cleanings, do not wait. Earlier intervention is always less expensive and less involved.

## What Happens If You Skip Cleanings for 1, 2, or 3+ Years?

We see this frequently, and there is no judgment in our office. But it helps to understand what accumulates during extended gaps:

**After 1 year:** Tartar buildup is noticeable in most patients. Early gingivitis may be present — reversible with cleaning and improved home care.

**After 2 years:** Calculus has hardened significantly and spreads further below the gumline. Shallow gum pockets may have deepened. Early bone loss is possible in susceptible patients.

**After 3+ years:** The probability of significant periodontal (gum) disease increases substantially. Patients who return after 3–5 years often need a deep cleaning (scaling and root planing, $600–$1,200 per quadrant) rather than a standard cleaning — a much more involved procedure.

**After 5+ years:** In many patients, we find moderate to severe periodontal disease, multiple new cavities, and existing restorations (fillings, crowns) that have failed or developed secondary decay. Treatment costs in this range often run into thousands of dollars.

The message is not to frighten you — it is to illustrate why the cost-benefit of a $0–$250 routine cleaning (often fully covered by insurance) is so strongly in your favor.

## Dental Cleanings for Children: How Often?

The same risk-based approach applies to children, with a few differences:

- Most children and teens benefit from **every 6 months**, as this is when we can catch early decay, monitor erupting teeth, and apply sealants.
- Children with **orthodontic appliances** (braces, expanders) typically benefit from **every 3–4 months** because brackets and wires trap plaque and increase cavity risk substantially.
- Children with **high cavity rates** — even in baby teeth — should be seen more frequently. The bacteria that cause cavities are transmissible and can establish early in children with certain dietary habits or anatomy.

The American Academy of Pediatric Dentistry recommends starting dental visits by age 1, or within 6 months of the first tooth erupting.

## Does Dental Insurance Cover More Frequent Cleanings?

Most dental insurance plans cover **two cleanings per year at 100%** as a preventive benefit. Whether they cover additional cleanings depends on your specific plan and your documented diagnosis.

**Good news:** Most insurance plans do cover periodontal maintenance cleanings (the 3–4 month cleanings for gum disease patients) — but they may require documentation of a periodontal diagnosis. We handle this paperwork on your behalf and will verify your specific benefits before scheduling.
